Advanced Data Analysis — Victorian & Global Drowning Trends

Key Knowledge — Advanced Data Context

  • Victoria's 54 drowning deaths in 2023–24 was the highest toll in over a decade
  • Non-fatal drownings were 60% above the 10-year average
  • CALD communities accounted for ~40% of deaths — highest proportion ever recorded
  • Globally, ~320,000 people drown annually (WHO 2021 data)
  • Low- and middle-income countries account for 90% of global drowning deaths
  • Males account for approximately 80% of global drowning deaths
  • Children under 5 are the highest-risk age group globally
  • In Australia, 1.2 people per 100,000 drown annually (RLSSA national data)

Sources: LSV — lsv.com.au/research/victorian-drowning-reports/ · WHO — who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/drowning · RLSSA — royallifesaving.com.au/research · CDC — cdc.gov/drowning/prevention

Physiology of Drowning — What Happens in the Body

Key Knowledge — Physiological Processes

  • Hypoxia: Oxygen deprivation. Brain cells begin dying within 4–6 minutes of oxygen loss. After 10 minutes without CPR, the chance of survival without significant brain damage is very low.
  • Cold water shock (1-10-1 rule): ~1 min to control breathing; ~10 min of useful swimming; up to 1 hr before hypothermia incapacitates (varies greatly by water temperature, body composition, and clothing).
  • Wet vs dry drowning: In most drowning cases (wet), water enters the lungs. In ~10–15% (dry), laryngospasm prevents water entry but still causes asphyxia.
  • Secondary drowning: Rare but real — symptoms (coughing, fatigue, chest pain) can appear hours after a near-drowning event. Always seek medical review after a submersion incident.
  • CPR physiology: Chest compressions create approximately 25–30% of normal cardiac output, delivering enough oxygenated blood to keep the brain alive until the heart can restart (spontaneously or via defibrillation).

Sources: St John Ambulance Vic — stjohnvic.com.au · Heart Foundation — heartfoundation.org.au · WHO Drowning Factsheet — who.int

Social Determinants of Drowning Risk

Key Knowledge — Who Is at Risk and Why

  • Social determinants are the non-medical factors that influence health outcomes
  • For drowning: gender norms, cultural background, socioeconomic status, and geography all play a role
  • CALD communities may have limited swimming opportunity due to cultural norms, cost, or country-of-origin practices
  • Masculinity norms ("don't back down from dares") are a documented risk factor for young male drowning
  • Rural and regional Victorians swim at unpatrolled inland waterways at higher rates than urban residents
  • People with lower socioeconomic status are less likely to have learned to swim as children
  • Alcohol is a factor in approximately 20–30% of adult drowning deaths in Victoria

Sources: LSV Drowning Report 2023–24 · RLSSA National Drowning Report · WHO Social Determinants of Health Framework

Evaluating Drowning Prevention Strategies

Key Knowledge — Prevention Approaches and Evidence

  • Engineering: Pool fencing (4-sided isolation) — strongest evidence base of any single intervention. Reduces child drowning in home pools by ~83%.
  • Supervision: Lifeguard presence at patrolled beaches — all 54 Victorian 2023–24 drowning deaths were at unpatrolled locations.
  • Education: Swim lessons — strong evidence for reducing child drowning. Adults benefit less unless combined with behaviour change.
  • Legislation: Pool fencing laws, lifejacket requirements, alcohol restrictions near water.
  • Technology: AEDs, throw rescue equipment, CCTV at public pools, drowning detection systems.
  • Community programs: LSV SwimSafe, RLSSA Swim and Survive, targeted CALD programs.

Sources: WHO Drowning Prevention Guide — who.int · LSV Toolkit — lsv.com.au/toolkit · RLSSA — royallifesaving.com.au
